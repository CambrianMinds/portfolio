Independent Policy Researcher · Author

Justin Bogner is the author of Autonomy, Capacity, and the Right to Exit, a policy packet arguing that Medical Aid in Dying eligibility should rest on a single principled standard — decisional capacity — rather than on clinical judgments about the quality or intensity of suffering.

The packet was researched and written independently, without institutional, governmental, or advocacy-organization sponsorship. It was first published in February 2026.

His work draws on bioethics, health-policy analysis, and constitutional and statutory law, and is addressed to legislators, clinicians, ethicists, advocates, and the academic community. The companion essay accompanying the packet documents the motivating personal experience behind the policy framework — included so readers can assess the argument with full knowledge of its origin, but written so the policy case stands on its own.

Justin lives and works in Huntington, Indiana.

Why This Work Exists

A Reform Argued From the Inside Out

Existing Medical Aid in Dying frameworks across North America make eligibility turn on judgments about the quality of a person's suffering. Those judgments are unequal in their application and incoherent on their own terms — a person with capacity is universally entitled to refuse life-sustaining treatment, but the same person, with the same capacity, can be denied access to active means.

This packet was written because no published work known to the author treats decisional capacity as the unifying criterion across both contexts, develops the procedural safeguards that follow from it, and addresses the strongest objections — disability-rights, depressive incapacity, slippery-slope, fiscal-rationale — in a single integrated argument.

The companion essay, available here, gives the personal context. Readers are encouraged to read it last; the policy case is structured to stand without it.
