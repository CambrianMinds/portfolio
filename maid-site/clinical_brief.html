Clinical Brief

A clinical and medical ethics brief on capacity-based MAiD.

This document provides a clinical perspective on the proposed capacity-based framework for Medical Aid in Dying (MAiD). It addresses the clinical incoherence of suffering-based criteria, the practical application of the two-stage capacity assessment protocol, and the management of complex cases, including those involving psychiatric diagnoses.

The Incoherence of "Suffering" as a Clinical Standard

Current MAiD frameworks often rely on the concept of "unbearable suffering" as a primary eligibility criterion. From a clinical standpoint, this presents significant challenges:

  • Subjectivity: Suffering is a subjective experience that is difficult to objectively measure or verify. This can lead to inconsistent and inequitable application of the law.
  • Performance Requirement: The need to demonstrate suffering can create a perverse incentive for patients to perform their distress, rather than honestly communicate their preferences.
  • Clinical Discomfort: Many clinicians are uncomfortable with the role of adjudicating a patient's subjective experience of suffering, a task for which they have little formal training.

The Two-Stage Capacity Assessment Protocol

The proposed framework replaces the suffering criterion with a robust, two-stage capacity assessment protocol. This protocol is designed to be both principled and practical, providing a clear and defensible standard for clinicians.

Stage 1: decisional capacity

The first stage involves a comprehensive assessment of the patient's decisional capacity. This includes an evaluation of their ability to:

  • Understand the nature and consequences of their decision.
  • Appreciate the reasonably foreseeable risks and benefits of all available options.
  • Communicate a stable and consistent choice over time.

Stage 2: Process Comprehension

The second stage assesses the patient's understanding of the MAiD process itself. This includes their understanding of the waiting period, the requirement for multiple independent assessments, and their right to withdraw their request at any time.

Managing Complex Cases

The proposed framework provides clear guidance for managing complex cases, including those involving psychiatric diagnoses. A history of mental illness does not automatically disqualify a patient from MAiD. Instead, the focus is on the patient's current decisional capacity. The protocol requires an independent psychiatric assessment for all patients with a history of mental illness to ensure that their decision is not the result of a treatable psychiatric condition.
